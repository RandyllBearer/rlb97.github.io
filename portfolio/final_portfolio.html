2. An example of how you made a choice related to color, fonts, captions, scale, or other aspects of the visual mode.

I do not belive I have a better example of visual revision than the changes I have made to my own website. For reference, below is a screenshot of my original website design:



And for reference is a screenshot of my current design as of this writing:



Ever since dropping the "Computer Scientist Blog" angle and going for one more personal, I decided to change my color scheme from a more subtly subdued to a more stylisticly explicit one. I unabashedly modelled it after the yet-to-be-officially-released Youtube Dark Theme (Please Google make it official soon). I've always preferred dark themes as they are easier on the eyes and as someone who spends a not-so-insignificant amount of time behind a computer screen it makes life much more comfortable.



While I was changing my color scheme I also wanted to fix a couple of design flaws with my original scheme, namely contrast. The sidebar text in my initial website design was a very light color on a not-so-dark background, making it difficult to read on most monitors. In my new dark theme it was much easier to pick a contrasting color as the majority of my colors are, well, dark black, and almost everything contrasts well with black. I also changed the design of the scrollbars on my website (except the .pdf scrollbars as I do not know how to change those) in order to create a more cohesive design.

3. An example of how you made a choice related to comparing one thing to another.

I believe my logo designs for Borders provide good examples of comparison between old and new. The prompt for this assignment was to revise the logo of a defunct company for the modern age, and the logo I chose (Borders) was a bit of an odd pick for this assignment as the Borders logo was very ahead of its time in the first place (Link to my presentation where I talk about this more indepth in the "Notes" section). Basically to summarize, Borders was founded in a time where other companies wanted to appeal to an earlier aesthetic, but Borders, as a purveyor of the newest music, the newest books, and the newest movies, wanted to appear to be as modern as possible. This is evident from their original logo design which strays heavily from other older logos such as The Hard Rock Cafe for example.



When comparing the old Borders logo (The topmost one in the above image) to other more modern logos I predominately noticed two things. One, the old logo, as a long horizontal rectange, is not well suited to mobile devices. Two, the logo lacks the "In the know" capacity of other modern logos such as Fed-Ex (Inverted Arrow), Google (Breaks the The Primary Colors), and Amazon (Delivers everything from A to Z). To solve the first issue I designed four potential mobile logos (square icons) for Borders. To solve the second, I made one of the corners of the Borders logo to be a book/laptop which breaks the boundaries of the box. This works on both a "Think outside the box level" and a "Our technology will let you break boundaries" level.
